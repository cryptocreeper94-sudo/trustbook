Over 30 bronze gears work together to track the cycles of the sun, moon, and planets.

**Out-of-Place Artifacts (OOPArts)**: Researchers like Michael Cremo have documented hundreds of anomalous artifacts that don't fit conventional timelines. # CHAPTER 6: DRAGONS, NOT DINOSAURS

The word "dinosaur" was invented in 1842 by Sir Richard Owen. He coined the term from Greek: "deinos" (terrible) + "sauros" (lizard). Before that, the creatures we now call dinosaurs were called something else.

Dragons.

## The Etymology and Historical Record

**Pre-1842 Documentation**:
The word "dragon" appears in virtually every ancient language:
- Hebrew: **tanniyn** (תַּנִּין) - appears 28 times in the Hebrew scriptures
- Greek: **drakon** (δράκων) - from which we get "dragon"
- Latin: **draco**
- Old English: **draca**
- Chinese: **lóng** (龍) - a category including various types

**Dictionary Definitions**:
Up until around 1946, Webster's Dictionary defined dragon as "a fabulous animal, generally represented as a monstrous winged serpent or lizard, with a crested head and large claws, and regarded as very powerful and ferocious."

Note: "fabulous" at that time meant "from fables" or "notable" - not "fake" or "imaginary." The 1828 Webster's actually described them as "now rare" - implying they still existed but were uncommon.

Scripture speaks of dragons as real creatures, not metaphors:

"Out of his mouth go burning lamps, and sparks of fire leap out. Out of his nostrils goes smoke, as out of a seething pot or caldron. His breath kindles coals, and a flame goes out of his mouth. In his neck remains strength... His heart is as firm as a stone; yea, as hard as a piece of the nether millstone."

This description includes:

- Smoke from nostrils
- Armored scales
- Extreme strength
